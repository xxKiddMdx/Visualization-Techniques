Good Visualizations

Visualization Analysis

  1. Who is the audience?
    • The audience is anybody, with any amount of visualization knowledge, reading about the gender pay gap.
  2. What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer?
    • This visualization asks and answers the question "What does the pay gap look like between men and women in certain occupations?".
  3. What data is encoded in the visualization?
    • The difference in pay between men and women in different occupations is encoded, as well as the salary and number of men/women working in this occupation.
  4. How does the visualization encode the data?
    • The visualization uses color to represent men and women. The length channel is used to represent the pay gap. Volume is used to encode number of men and women working in each field, as well as position.
  5. What tasks readers perform on the visualization ?
    • Readers can identify which occupations have the largest pay gaps, and look at trends to understand how the pay gap grows when salary rises. Similarly, they can also identify the trend that there is less of a pay gap in female dominated fields.
  6. How are the five principles applied to this visualization?
    • Truthfulness is used in this visualization through accurately representing the axes and by having a straight on view, rather than angled down to make the pay gap line appear longer. This is very functional because the way it encodes the data makes the trends very easy to read, supports meaningful tasks, and allows us to answer question about the topic. This graph is beautiful because it is visually aesthetic, is free of unecessary elements, and supports additional subjective goals. This is insightful as well because it informs us about our society. Lastly, this is enlightening because through its interactive abilities, it engages with the audience and conveys a compelling story.
  7. Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad?
    • This is a good visualization because it allows viewers to easily grasp trends about the data and topic through functional and beautiful visualization techniques.

Visualization Analysis

  1. Who is the audience?
    • People who are interested in the American diet.
  2. What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer?
    • The visualization aims to show how Americans' propensity for protein sources changed over time.
  3. What data is encoded in the visualization?
    • The percentage of a specific type of food of all protein sources.
  4. How does the visualization encode the data?
    • It encodes the data by using a horizontal bar chart.
  5. What tasks readers perform on the visualization ?
    • Readers are able to compare the percentages of different types of food in different periods of time. They can also see the trend by following the words: chicken rises to the top between 2004 and 2019. Although the pounds per capita of beef decreased, beef still makes up a large portion of protein consumption.
  6. How are the five principles applied to this visualization?
    • Truthful: By comparing each type of food to the total protein consumption, readers can easily find the trend that more people start to consume chicken. The data itself is also valid since the author mentioned that the source is from the USDA Food Availability Data System.
    • Functional: The visualization is very understandable and easy to follow. It uses a sentence in the chart to guide the reader to find the key information. The chart also uses the percentage to make it easier for readers to compare.
    • Beautiful: The color selection of this visualization is very precise and beautiful. All colors are symbolic so that readers can easily associate colors with the actual food.
    • Insightful: The chart helps me to confirm my belief that beef is very popular in the United States. However, it is surprising to see that chicken is the most popular protein source right now.
    • Enlightening: It can be helpful for restaurants since they are able to adjust their menus based on the American diet.
  7. Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad?
    • It is a good visualization since it not only provides accurate data but also utilizes an interesting way to interact with readers and track the changes over the years. The result that readers can get from the visualization can be insightful and easy to memorize.

Bad Visualization 1

Visualization Analysis

  1. Who is the audience?
    • The audience is members of the company that this data comes from.
  2. What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer?
    • This is supposed to convey how many overtime hours employees work per month, but it does a bad job answering this question.
  3. What data is encoded in the visualization?
    • The number of overtime hours per month is encoded in this visualization.
  4. How does the visualization encode the data?
    • The visualization uses color (I am not sure for what purpose though), length (although it is used in a confusing way) and data labels. It uses a horizontal bar chart configuration style.
  5. What tasks readers perform on the visualization?
    • Readers determine overtime hours per month, finding trends and extremes.
  6. How are the five principles applied to this visualization?
    • This is truthful because it doesn't try to portray the data in a deceitful way, it is not very functional because it encodes data in a confusing way, making it look like hours are increasing each month. Beatifulness is enhanced through color. This is not very insightful because the reader has a hard time in finding trends/extremes, nor is it enlightening because it doesn't inform readers well or make data more clear.
  7. Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad?
    • This visualization is bad because it doesn't use the appropriate marks and channels to display information, leaving the visualization hard to read and gain insight from.

Redesigns

Redesign 1: 2nd place

Although I don't think this is the best redesign choice here, I really like this redesign technique because it is creative and something that I haven't seen much before. I it is very similar to the bar chart, using length as the main visual encoding, but it lays out the bars in a circular fashion. Compared to the original, it is different because it uses length as the encoding channel for the number of hours (a quantitive measurement), rather than for the month which is a categorical/nominal element. Length is used as the main source of comparison here, which works very well to compare overtime hours. Color/hue could be added to double encode this quantitive data. In this specific redesign, I traded creativity/beautifulness with readability and functionality, because it is harder to determine which month is where due to the radial axis of months.

Redesign 2: 1st place Winner!

Much like in redesign 1, I used length to represent the quantitative value of number of overtime hours worked, for the same reasons as stated for redesign 1. This improves upon the original because it is much more functional- the data/trend are much easier to read when we encode the quantitative data with length becase it is very easy to compare accross. While I think this is the most effective redesign because it is the easiest to observe trends from and find extremes, it is sort of boring. Color/saturation and data labels could be used to add another visual encoding channel for the quantiative data to increase the beauty of the design.

Redesign 3: 3rd place

This redesign uses position and volume to visually encode the number of overtime hours worked. I chose to use both because I thought either of these visualization channels on it's own would not be readable enough, but with both it adds functionality and reiterates the trends. This is very different from the first one and adds 2 visual encoding technqiues that were not used before, and it switches the x and y axis. This improves upon the original because position is a more effective way of encoding quantitative data. With just position encoding it, I thought the visualization was not beautiful or engaging enough, so I added the volume visual channel to add a new level of depth and engagement.

Bad Visualization 3

Visualization Analysis

  1. Who is the audience?
    • People who are interested in emoji usage in the daily chat
  2. What message and questions does this visualization convey and answer?
    • This visualization aims to show how frequently emojis are used
  3. What data is encoded in the visualization?
    • The frequency of usage of emojis.
  4. How does the visualization encode the data?
    • It encodes the data by using a horizontal bar chart.
  5. What tasks readers perform on the visualization?
    • Readers are able to compare the frequencies of different emojis.
  6. How are the five principles applied to this visualization?
    • Truthful: We can not determine the accuracy of the data since the author mentioned that data came from various sources. It’s also hard for readers to verify the accuracy since there is no software that is able to record all the chats.
    • Functional: The visualization is hard to follow since there are so many divisions in the pie chart, and the lines are too thin that readers need to spend more time connecting the emoji to the corresponding area.
    • Beautiful: The chart uses random colors to represent emojis, which are not symbolic at all. Also, the author uses the same color more than once, which makes the chart even harder to follow.
    • Insightful: It does not provide much meaningful information other than telling readers what are the most popular emojis.
    • Enlightening: The visualization does not show any further effects after knowing the rank.
  7. Considering all aspects, why is this visualization good or bad?
    • I think this is a bad visualization because it divides the chart into too many parts and picks colors randomly. Besides, the sources of the data are not authoritative, and the chart itself does not provide much meaningful information.

Redesigns

Redesign 1: 1st place Winner!

I think my first redesign improves the most from the original. By using a bar chart instead of a pie chart, the visualization shows the ranking more efficiently and directly. In addition, I decided to include only the top 8 popular emojis such that readers can grasp the most important information in a short time. Most of the audience is unwilling to spend much time searching for the frequency of a specific emoji that’s not common in use.

Redesign 2: 2nd place

I make the original bar chart simpler and more understandable by categorize emojis such that reader may spend less time to get the main idea of the graph. However, the color selection can still be improved.

Redesign 3: 3rd place

This redesign adjusts the size of emoji based on their percentage. Readers can easily find emojis that are the most popular. However, it’s hard to compare emojis of similar popularity because they are about the same size.
